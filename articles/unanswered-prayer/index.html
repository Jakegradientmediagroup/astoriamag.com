I Prayed the Same Prayer for Three Years Before I Understood Why It Wasn't Being Answered

By Renata Fields · 11 min read · Mind & Spirit
✦

For three years, I prayed the same prayer almost every single night. Not a general one — a specific one, with specific words, asking for a specific outcome I won't detail here because it's not really the point of this story. What matters is that I asked, consistently, sincerely, for three years, and as far as I could tell, nothing happened. Not a no. Not a clear redirection. Just silence, repeated nightly, for three years.

I want to tell you what I eventually understood about that silence, because I don't think it's what most of the well-meaning advice around unanswered prayer actually says. And I think a lot of women reading this are in their own version of year two or year three of asking for something that hasn't come, wondering if the problem is their faith, their worthiness, or whether anyone is listening at all.

The Explanations I Was Given, and Why None of Them Actually Helped

I heard all the standard ones. "God's timing isn't our timing." "Maybe the answer is no." "Maybe you're not ready for what you're asking for." Each of these is theologically defensible, and each one, delivered to a woman in the middle of an actual three-year silence, lands somewhere between unhelpful and quietly cruel. They're the kind of thing said by people who are not currently in the silence themselves, which is most people's experience of other people's crises.

What nobody said to me, and what I wish someone had, is that the question "why isn't this prayer being answered" is often the wrong question, because it assumes the prayer is a vending machine transaction — input request, output result — rather than what I now believe prayer actually is, which is considerably stranger and slower than that.

The Uncomfortable Realization: I Wasn't Actually Asking What I Thought I Was Asking

Here is the part of this story that took me the longest to admit, even to myself. Somewhere around month eighteen, in a moment of genuine exhaustion rather than any particular spiritual insight, I finally asked myself a question I'd been avoiding: what was I actually asking for, underneath the specific words? And the honest answer embarrassed me. I wasn't asking for the thing itself. I was asking for permission to stop being afraid without having to do the work of becoming someone less afraid. I wanted the external circumstance to change so that my internal state would change as a side effect, without my having to touch the internal state directly.

I don't think this is a unique confession. I think an enormous amount of what we call prayer, especially prayer repeated identically over long stretches of time, is actually this: a request for the world to rearrange itself around an unexamined fear, rather than a request to be changed ourselves. And I think this matters because a prayer built on that foundation can genuinely go unanswered for years, not because God is withholding, but because the actual thing being requested — permission to stay the same while circumstances shift — isn't really available. It was never on offer.

"I had been asking God to change my circumstances so that I wouldn't have to change. Nobody warns you that this particular prayer can go unanswered indefinitely, because it was never really answerable in the form I was asking it."

What Actually Started to Shift, and When

The shift, when it came, wasn't dramatic. It wasn't a sign or a vision or a moment of sudden clarity in a church service. It was smaller and much less satisfying as a story: I started asking a different, harder question in the same nightly moment where I used to ask the old one. Instead of asking for the circumstance to change, I started asking what I was supposed to become in the middle of it not changing. That's a much less comfortable prayer to pray, because it doesn't let you off the hook the way the old one did. It doesn't promise relief. It asks something of you.

I won't pretend that this new question got answered quickly either, in the sense of producing a neat conclusion I can hand you here. What I can tell you is that something about the waiting itself became different. It stopped feeling like a holding pattern outside my real life, and started feeling like it might actually be my real life, the one I was supposed to be paying attention to rather than waiting to get past.

What I Think Silence in Prayer Is Actually For

I've come to believe, and I want to be careful here because this is belief rather than certainty, that the silence itself does something that an immediate answer wouldn't. An immediate yes would have let me keep the version of myself that made the request in the first place — afraid, unchanged, waiting for external rescue. The silence, stretched out over years, did something an instant answer couldn't: it forced me to keep living, keep making decisions, keep building a life, without the thing I thought I needed in order to start living fully. And somewhere in that stretch, I discovered that I had actually been living fully the whole time, just without noticing, because I'd been so fixated on the specific thing I hadn't gotten.

The kind of prayer that tends to get "answered" faster

In my own experience and in conversations with other women who've been through long silences of their own, the prayers that seem to resolve faster tend to be the ones aimed at internal transformation rather than external rearrangement. Not because God prefers one over the other in some transactional sense, but because internal transformation is actually available to participate in, in a way that external circumstances often aren't. You can become less afraid. You cannot always make the job appear, the person return, the diagnosis reverse, the timeline accelerate.

The kind of prayer that tends to stretch on

The prayers that seem to stretch the longest, in my observation, are the ones asking the world to solve an internal problem from the outside. Not because those prayers are wrong to pray — I don't believe that, and I don't think you should stop asking for the things you actually want. I think they stretch longer because the real work being requested, even if we don't realize we're requesting it, is internal, and internal work simply takes the time it takes.

A Provocative Thing I Now Believe, Which I Didn't Believe Three Years Ago

I now think some silences in prayer are not God withholding an answer. They're God waiting for us to ask a truer question than the one we started with. This is an uncomfortable thing to believe, because it puts some of the responsibility for the waiting back on us, rather than leaving it entirely in the hands of divine timing, which is a much more comfortable place to leave it. But I've found it to be more honest, and more useful, than the alternative.

I want to be careful not to turn this into a universal law, because I've also sat with women whose prayers for healing, for a child, for a marriage to survive, went unanswered in ways that had nothing to do with an unexamined internal question, and everything to do with living in a world where suffering doesn't always resolve into a lesson. I'm not offering a theory of everything here. I'm offering what I learned from my own specific three years, which may or may not map onto yours.

What I'd Say to a Woman in Her Own Year Two

If you're in the middle of your own long silence right now, praying the same words you've prayed for months or years, here's the only thing I feel qualified to offer, having been there myself: ask, gently and without judgment toward yourself, what you're actually requesting underneath the specific words. Not to shame yourself out of the prayer — keep praying it, if it's still true. But ask whether there's a harder, truer question sitting just beneath the one you've been repeating, and whether you've been avoiding it because the answer to that one actually requires something of you.

You may find, as I did, that the silence wasn't punishment, and it wasn't neglect. It was the space where the real question finally had room to surface, if you were willing to let it.

A Closing Word

Three years is a long time to ask for one thing. It is also, I've come to believe, exactly as long as it needed to take — not because God moves slowly on purpose, but because I moved slowly, and the prayer waited for me to catch up to a truer version of what I actually needed to ask.

What Research on Religious Coping Actually Suggests

Psychologists who study religious coping — how people use faith to process hardship and uncertainty — have found something that maps closely onto my own experience, even though I didn't discover the research until years after living through it. There's a documented difference between what's sometimes called "deferring" religious coping, where a person hands the entire problem over to God and takes a passive stance while waiting, and "collaborative" religious coping, where a person continues actively engaging — making decisions, doing the internal work, staying in relationship with the difficulty — while also trusting something larger than themselves. The collaborative style is consistently associated with better outcomes: less anxiety during the waiting, more resilience, and paradoxically, often faster resolution, not because prayer is a transaction but because the person doing the collaborative work tends to be more prepared to recognize and receive an answer when it does arrive.

My three years, in retrospect, moved from a deferring posture — asking and then simply waiting, passively, for the external world to shift — toward something closer to a collaborative one, where I was still asking, but also doing the harder internal work alongside the asking. I don't think this is coincidental to when things actually began to shift.

A Longer Look at What "Answered" Even Means

Part of what kept me stuck for so long was an unexamined assumption about what an answered prayer was even supposed to look like. I'd pictured a specific external event — a door opening, a circumstance resolving, a clear yes. What I didn't consider, for a long time, was the possibility that the answer might not be an event at all, but a slow change in me that made the original request less urgent, not because I'd gotten what I wanted, but because I'd become someone who needed it less.

This is a deeply unsatisfying answer if you're in the middle of the waiting and want something concrete to point to. I understand that completely, because I was that person for years. But I've come to think that some of the most significant answers to prayer aren't events we can point to at all. They're the quiet fact that we're different people than we were when we started asking, and that difference is itself the response, even when it doesn't look like one from the outside.

How to tell the difference, in practice

One imperfect but useful test I've developed: ask whether you'd still want the original thing, exactly as you first imagined it, if it arrived tomorrow. If the honest answer has changed — if you'd want it differently, or want something adjacent instead, or find that the original want has quietly reorganized itself — that's often a sign the internal work has been happening, even if the external circumstance hasn't moved an inch.

What I'd Tell My Own Daughter About This, Someday

If my daughter comes to me one day in her own version of this — some prayer she's repeated for months or years without any visible movement — I don't think I'll tell her to just keep waiting, and I don't think I'll tell her God has a secret plan she isn't allowed to understand yet. I think I'll ask her the same question that finally moved something in me: what are you actually asking for, underneath the words you keep repeating? And I'll tell her that the answer to that question is sometimes harder to sit with than the original silence was, but that it's usually where the real movement starts.

A Closing Reflection on Time Itself

There's a specific kind of grief in a long, unanswered prayer that I don't think gets named often enough: the grief of time passing while you wait. Three years is not an abstraction. It's three years of birthdays, three years of ordinary Tuesdays, three years you cannot get back, spent in a posture of waiting for something that hadn't yet arrived. I think it's honest to grieve that time, even after the waiting has resolved into something meaningful. The meaning doesn't erase the cost of the years themselves, and I don't think it's supposed to. What I've settled into is holding both at once — genuine gratitude for what I eventually understood, and genuine acknowledgment that three years is a real span of a real life, not a footnote on the way to a lesson.
