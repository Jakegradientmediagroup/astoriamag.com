My Daughter Told Me I Was “Too Much” and It Broke Something Open I Didn't Expect

By Kate Brennan · 6 min read · Motherhood
❋

My daughter was twelve when she told me, in the middle of an ordinary argument about a curfew that wasn't even a real curfew yet, that I was "too much." Not too strict. Not too unfair. Too much — the exact phrase, word for word, that I had spent my entire life hearing about myself, from teachers, from old boyfriends, eventually from a marriage that didn't survive it.

I want to be honest about what happened in my body in that moment, because I don't think enough parenting writing tells the truth about how much a child's words can land exactly where an old wound already lives. I didn't respond as her mother in that moment. I responded as the twelve-year-old, the twenty-two-year-old, and the thirty-five-year-old who had all separately been told the same thing, and it took everything I had not to shrink in front of my own child the way I'd learned to shrink in front of everyone else who'd ever said it.

The Question I Forced Myself to Ask Before Responding

I didn't answer her right away, which is not typical for me — typically I respond fast, sometimes too fast. But something made me stop and ask myself a genuinely uncomfortable question before I said anything back: where, specifically, did she learn that phrase? Not the general concept of a parent being frustrating. The exact words. Twelve-year-olds don't usually invent phrasing like "too much" from nothing. They absorb it from somewhere.

The honest answer, once I let myself sit with it, was devastating: she'd learned it from me. Not directed at her — directed at myself, out loud, more times than I'd ever tracked, in the ordinary self-deprecating way so many women narrate their own personalities. "Sorry, I know I'm too much." "Ignore me, I get too intense about things." I had spent years handing my daughter, in real time, a script for how to describe a woman who takes up any space at all.

"I had been teaching her the exact language to use against me, one offhand self-criticism at a time, for years, without ever noticing I was doing it."

What I Actually Said to Her, Once I Understood This

I didn't punish her for the phrase, which was my first instinct, buried under the sting of it. Instead, a day later, once I'd actually processed my own reaction, I told her the truth: that "too much" was a phrase I'd heard about myself my whole life, that I'd unknowingly taught it to her by using it about myself, and that I wanted us both to stop using it — not because feelings and frustration weren't allowed, but because "too much" wasn't actually a description of a person. It was a judgment dressed up as one, and it had never once, in my entire life, come from someone who actually had my best interest at heart.

The Uncomfortable Pattern I Had to Face in Myself

Here's what I think a lot of parenting content skips over: the phrases our children use against us in an argument are rarely original. They're mirrors. My daughter didn't invent a critique of me out of nowhere. She reflected back, with the sharp precision only a child can manage, language I'd been modeling for her about myself for years. If I wanted her to stop using it, the actual work wasn't a conversation about respect or tone. It was an audit of my own self-talk, conducted in front of her, for years, without my awareness that she was cataloging every word of it.

What I Started Doing Differently

Catching the self-deprecation out loud

I started noticing, in real time, every instance of describing myself as too much, too intense, too anything, in front of my kids. Not eliminating the feeling underneath — sometimes I genuinely am overwhelmed by my own intensity in a given moment — but changing the language around it. Instead of "sorry, I'm too much," I started saying "I'm feeling a lot right now," which is both more accurate and doesn't hand a child a permanent character judgment to file away about me.

Naming the pattern directly with her

I told my daughter explicitly what I'd noticed — that I thought she'd learned the phrase from me, and that I was working on not using it about myself anymore. This wasn't a lecture about her behavior. It was an admission about mine, offered honestly, which I think landed very differently than a correction would have.

What Happened a Few Months Later

The real test came a few months after that first conversation, during another argument, when I felt the old phrase rising in my own throat — the urge to preemptively describe myself as too much before she could say it first, a kind of defensive self-deprecation I'd been doing my whole life without naming it as a habit at all. I caught it, said something else instead, and watched her not reach for the phrase either. I don't think that was an accident.

A Provocative Thought About Where "Too Much" Actually Comes From

I've come to believe that "too much" is almost never a genuine description of a person's actual personality. It's a phrase reserved almost exclusively for women and girls who take up space, express big feelings, or ask for things directly, and it's rarely aimed at men doing the identical behavior. I don't think my daughter absorbed this gendered double standard from nowhere either. I think she absorbed it the same way she absorbed the phrase itself — by watching how the world, including me, talked about women who were exactly as intense as I actually am.

A Closing Word

My daughter is thirteen now. She still gets big feelings, and so do I, and neither of us currently uses that particular phrase about the other, or about ourselves, nearly as often as we used to. I don't think I fixed this entirely. I think I noticed it in time to interrupt a pattern that was already three generations deep in my own family before it became a fourth. That feels like the actual win, more than any single argument I've ever successfully de-escalated.

What Research on Self-Talk Modeling Actually Shows

Developmental researchers who study how children absorb self-concept from their parents have found that children learn far more from overheard self-talk than from direct instruction. A mother who tells her daughter "you are wonderful exactly as you are" while also routinely describing herself as too much, too loud, or too intense is, in practice, teaching two contradictory lessons simultaneously, and children reliably absorb the modeled behavior over the stated instruction. This tracks exactly with what happened in my house. I could have given my daughter a thousand verbal affirmations about her worth, and she still would have learned, more powerfully, from watching how I described myself when I thought no one was really listening.

A Closing Word on the Three Generations Before Her

My own mother used to say she was too much, and her mother before her, as far as I can trace it back. I don't think any of us were actually too much for anything. I think we were women with real feelings and real intensity, living in a culture that had a ready-made phrase for punishing exactly that in women, and we each, without meaning to, handed the phrase down to the next generation like an inheritance nobody asked for. I'd like to be the one who finally doesn't pass it on.
