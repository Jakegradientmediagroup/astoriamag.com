I Thought My Anxiety Meant I Didn't Trust God Enough. A Therapist Finally Corrected Me

By Renata Fields · 5 min read · Mind & Spirit
◈

For most of my twenties and thirties, I believed my anxiety was fundamentally a faith problem. Scripture said not to be anxious about anything. I was anxious about nearly everything. The math, as I understood it, was simple and damning: my anxiety was evidence that my trust in God was insufficient, and the solution was to pray harder, believe more completely, and will the anxiety away through sheer spiritual effort.

It took a licensed therapist, not a pastor, to finally correct this belief, and I want to walk through what she said, because I think an enormous number of women in faith communities are quietly carrying the same damaging equation I was.

The Verse I'd Weaponized Against Myself

"Do not be anxious about anything" had become, in my mind, less a comfort and more an indictment. Every time an anxious thought showed up, I heard two things simultaneously: the anxious thought itself, and a second, harsher voice telling me that having the thought at all was proof of spiritual failure. This doubled the weight of every anxious moment. I wasn't just anxious. I was anxious and ashamed of being anxious, which is a considerably heavier thing to carry than anxiety alone.

What My Therapist Actually Said

When I finally described this pattern to her, expecting either sympathy or some clinical workaround, she said something that rearranged years of quiet self-punishment in a single sentence: anxiety is a physiological response, generated by a nervous system doing exactly what nervous systems are built to do, and treating a physiological response as a referendum on the quality of your faith is like treating a fever as a referendum on your character. The fever means your body is responding to something. It doesn't mean you're a bad person for having a body that responds.

"My anxiety was not a crack in my faith. It was a nervous system, doing its job, in a woman who also happened to have faith. The two were never actually in competition."

The Theology I Had Never Actually Examined

Once I sat with this reframe, I went back to the passages I'd been weaponizing against myself and noticed something I'd missed for years: nowhere do they say anxious feelings are forbidden or sinful. They say something closer to an invitation — bring the anxiety somewhere, rather than eliminate it through willpower before it's allowed to exist. I had been reading an invitation to bring my anxiety to God as though it were a command to not have anxiety in the first place, which is a very different, and much crueler, instruction.

Why This Distinction Actually Matters

This might sound like a small semantic difference, but I've come to believe it's the difference between a sustainable spiritual life and one built on constant, low-grade shame. A woman who believes her anxiety proves insufficient faith will spend enormous energy trying to suppress or hide a normal human experience, and will feel like a spiritual failure every single time it doesn't work, which is often, because suppression is not how nervous systems actually resolve activation. A woman who understands anxiety as a physiological signal she's allowed to bring into her faith, rather than a mark against it, can actually do the work of addressing it — through therapy, through practical coping tools, through prayer used as genuine comfort rather than a magic wand expected to eliminate the feeling on command.

What Changed Once I Stopped Treating Anxiety as Sin

I stopped hiding it

Once I no longer believed my anxiety was evidence against my faith, I stopped hiding it from the people in my faith community, which meant I finally started getting real support instead of performing a calm I didn't feel.

I actually sought clinical help

For years I'd resisted therapy specifically because some part of me believed needing it meant my faith wasn't strong enough to handle things on its own. Once that belief dissolved, getting actual clinical support stopped feeling like a spiritual failure and started feeling like basic stewardship of the body I'd been given.

My prayer life actually changed

Instead of praying for the anxiety to disappear entirely, which rarely worked and left me feeling more spiritually inadequate each time it didn't, I started praying while anxious, bringing the actual feeling into the room rather than waiting to pray until I felt calm enough to seem like a person with sufficient faith.

A Provocative Thought About Faith Communities and Mental Health

I've come to believe that a lot of well-meaning faith culture has quietly, unintentionally taught women that mental health struggles are spiritual failures in disguise, and that this belief does genuine damage, keeping women from clinical help they need while adding a layer of shame to an already difficult experience. I don't think this was ever the intention behind the teaching. I think it's what happens when a verse about bringing worry to God gets flattened into a command to not have worry at all.

What I'd Say to a Woman Carrying This Same Belief

If you've spent years treating your anxiety as evidence of insufficient faith, I want to offer you the same correction that took a therapist, not a pastor, to finally give me: your nervous system is not a referendum on your relationship with God. Getting professional help for an anxious mind is not a failure to trust Him sufficiently. It might, in fact, be one of the more faithful things you can do with the body and mind you've been given.

A Closing Word

I still have anxiety. I likely always will, given my particular nervous system and a family history I can't undo. What I no longer have is the second layer — the shame that used to sit on top of every anxious moment, insisting it meant something damning about my faith. That layer is gone, and its absence alone has made an enormous difference in how sustainable my faith actually feels day to day.
