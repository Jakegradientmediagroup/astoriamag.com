The Summer Events Redefining What Christian Community Looks Like

By Rachel Monroe · 5 min read · Faith & Community

Something has shifted in how Christian women in their thirties and forties are choosing to gather. The megachurch model — large, efficient, anonymizing — is losing ground to something smaller and more intentional. The women driving this shift are not disenchanted with faith. They are disenchanted with the performance of community: the programmatic small group that never gets past surface-level, the women's event designed to feel inspiring rather than transforming.

The Retreat as Spiritual Reset

The multi-day retreat has gained significant traction: small by design, immersive, built around silence and Scripture and genuine conversation rather than performance and programming. The common thread in what women report taking away isn't the speaker or the curriculum. It's the relationships formed in the in-between moments — over meals, on morning walks, in the quiet hours after the formal sessions end.

The Rise of the Intimate Dinner

In Nashville, in Dallas, in Charleston and Denver and a dozen other cities, a specific format has been quietly proliferating: the dinner hosted by a woman of faith for eight to twelve other women, centered on a real question, in a beautiful space, with real food and no agenda beyond connection. No merchandise. No speaker fee. Just the ancient practice of women gathering around a table to wrestle with something that matters.

"The most transformative Christian community I've experienced has always happened in rooms small enough that nobody could hide."

What to Look for If You're Trying to Find Your People

Start locally — most cities have women's Bible study groups that operate independently of a megachurch structure, often through word of mouth. Ask the women you most admire where they're finding genuine community. Attend one retreat, even if it requires travel, and pay attention to what the experience shows you about what's possible.

Community is only half the equation — the other half is what you do with your own quiet hours, which is exactly what we explore in on Sabbath, silence, and the radical act of resting well.

The Uncomfortable Truth About Why the Megachurch Model Is Losing Ground

It's worth naming directly what's actually driving women away from the megachurch model, beyond simple preference: a growing discomfort with the scale and, in some well-publicized cases, the institutional failures that scale can enable when accountability gets diffused across large, professionalized organizations. Smaller, more intimate gatherings aren't just aesthetically preferred. For many women, they represent a genuine, considered response to feeling let down by larger institutions that grew faster than their accountability structures could support.

The Provocative Part: Intimacy Isn't Automatically Safer

It's tempting to assume that small, intimate faith gatherings are automatically healthier than large institutional ones, but small groups carry their own risks — less oversight, more potential for an unhealthy dynamic or a charismatic but unaccountable leader to go unchecked, simply because there are fewer eyes on the group and less formal structure to catch problems early. Size isn't the actual variable that determines health. Accountability is, and it's worth evaluating any gathering, large or small, by that standard specifically rather than by its size alone.

"A small gathering with no accountability isn't inherently safer than a large one. It's just harder for outsiders to see the problem before it's already gone quite far."

Questions Worth Asking Before You Commit to a New Community

Who has authority here, and who holds them accountable?

Every healthy gathering, regardless of size, has a clear answer to this question. If the honest answer is "no one, really," that's worth taking seriously as a warning sign rather than a feature of the intimacy.

What happens when someone disagrees or leaves?

How a community responds to dissent or departure tells you more about its actual health than anything said in a welcome conversation. Healthy communities allow both. Unhealthy ones treat either as a betrayal requiring social consequence.

What Real Community Actually Costs

The intimate dinners and retreats this piece describes are genuinely valuable, and they're also, often, a real financial and time commitment that not every woman can access equally. It's worth being honest that some of the most meaningful faith community happens in far less curated settings — a weekly phone call, a modest local Bible study, a neighbor who shows up consistently — and that the retreat-and-dinner-party version of community, however lovely, isn't the only legitimate version available.

What Sociologists of Religion Have Documented

Researchers studying the shift away from megachurch attendance consistently find that the drivers are less about theology and more about a broader cultural shift toward smaller, more accountable institutions across many domains — not unique to faith communities at all. The same generation moving toward intimate dinner-party faith gatherings is also moving away from large employers toward smaller companies, and away from mass media toward niche communities. This is a broader pattern of institutional distrust, and it's worth understanding this shift as part of something larger than a faith-specific trend.

A Reflection Before Joining a New Community

Beyond warmth and shared values, it's worth asking directly: what does this community do when someone within it causes harm? The answer to this single question, asked plainly before you invest years of belonging, reveals more about a community's actual health than any number of beautiful dinners or retreats ever could.

A Closing Word on Belonging Without Losing Discernment

The desire for genuine community is one of the healthiest instincts a person can have, and it's also, precisely because it's so strong, one of the instincts most vulnerable to being exploited by a community that isn't actually healthy. Holding both truths at once — a real, urgent desire for belonging, and a real, sober discernment about where that belonging is offered — is the actual skill this entire piece is pointing toward, and it's a skill worth taking seriously rather than assuming warmth alone is sufficient evidence of health.
