The Conversations Every Couple Needs to Have Before Year Five

By Priya Holloway · 8 min read · Relationships & Marriage

In fifteen years of working as a marriage therapist, I have sat with hundreds of couples in the middle of crises that were, almost without exception, preventable. Not because the crises themselves were predictable, but because the underlying disconnects that made those crises catastrophic had been building, quietly and unaddressed, for years.

The Money Conversation You're Actually Avoiding

Most couples have some version of a money conversation before marriage. Very few have the real one. The real one isn't about the budget or who pays which bill. It's about what money means to each of you — what it represents, what it triggers, what your parents taught you about it without meaning to. A couple where one person secretly equates financial security with love and the other equates financial generosity with control will fight about money constantly without ever once fighting about money.

"Every recurring argument in a marriage is a surface argument. The real argument is always about something beneath it — usually a need that hasn't been named."

The Parenting Philosophy You've Only Half-Discussed

You may have agreed on whether you want children, how many, and roughly when. What most couples haven't discussed is what kind of parents they actually intend to be — and specifically, how their own childhoods will inform that, because they will. Discipline approaches. Faith and its role in how children are raised. These conversations are slow and sometimes uncomfortable. Have them before you're sitting across from each other at a pediatrician's office having them for the first time under pressure.

What You Each Need That You're Not Asking For

After the first year or two of marriage, most people settle into a pattern of either asking for what they need and being told it's too much, or not asking at all and growing quietly resentful. The antidote is a conversation about what you each actually need to feel loved and seen, conducted at a time when no one is already frustrated.

The Career and Ambition Conversation

People change. The woman who was happy to deprioritize her career in her twenties may feel very differently at thirty-five. Check in on this annually — not as an audit, but as a genuine question: Is this arrangement still working for both of us? What does the next five years look like if we're being honest?

Many of these conversations start even earlier than year five — back when you're still deciding what standards you're willing to hold in dating in the first place.
