First Home or Fifth: The Questions Every Smart Woman Asks Her Realtor

By Dana Whitmore · 5 min read · Real Estate & Home

Real estate transactions are among the largest financial decisions most people will ever make, and they are conducted in an environment specifically designed to create urgency and suppress deliberation. Everybody in the room has a financial incentive that may or may not align with yours. This is a reason to be prepared.

Questions for Your Buyer's Agent

"What's the actual price per square foot for comparable properties sold in the last 90 days?"

Not listed. Sold. Listing prices are aspirations. Sale prices are data. Any agent worth working with can pull this in five minutes and should offer it before you make an offer.

"Why are the sellers moving?"

Motivated sellers negotiate differently than sellers testing the market. Your agent may not always know, but asking the question reveals how much information they've bothered to gather.

"What are this neighborhood's long-term trajectory and most significant risk factors?"

A good agent has opinions about where neighborhoods are heading and is willing to share them honestly. If they answer every version of this question with unqualified enthusiasm, they're telling you they prioritize the sale over your interests.

"The right question isn't 'should I buy this house?' It's 'what would I need to know to feel confident about this decision?' Then find out everything on that list."

Questions for the Inspection

Ask the inspector to walk you through the roof, HVAC, and foundation in plain language. These are the three systems most likely to produce a large, unplanned expense in the first five years. Ask what they'd do if it were their house.

The Question Nobody Asks

"If I buy this property and need to sell it in three years, what's my realistic outcome?" This forces a realistic conversation about liquidity and market risk that most buyers never have because they're too emotionally invested by the time they're considering an offer. You need to know before the closing, not after.

The Uncomfortable Truth About Who Your Agent Actually Works For

Here's something rarely said plainly at the outset of a real estate relationship: in most transactions, your agent's commission is a percentage of the sale price, which means their financial incentive is modestly but genuinely misaligned with yours — they benefit from a higher price and a faster close, you benefit from the opposite on both counts. This doesn't make agents dishonest as a rule. It means the incentive structure is worth understanding clearly, rather than assuming your agent's interests and yours are perfectly identical throughout the process.

The Provocative Question Nobody Asks in the First Meeting

"How many other transactions are you currently juggling, and where does mine rank in your actual attention?" Most buyers never ask this, out of politeness, and then discover mid-transaction that their agent is stretched across a dozen active deals with limited bandwidth for any single one. A direct answer to this question, asked early, tells you more about the service you'll actually receive than almost anything else you could ask.

"Politeness has cost more buyers a good outcome than almost any other single factor in the process. Ask the uncomfortable question early."

What the Inspection Report Won't Tell You Directly

The inspector's own liability limits their language

Most inspection reports are written in deliberately hedged language — "may indicate," "could suggest" — because inspectors carry liability insurance and write defensively. Learning to read past the hedging, or asking the inspector directly and verbally what they'd personally do in your position, gets you a far more useful answer than the written report alone.

What gets excluded from a standard inspection

Sewer lines, chimneys, pools, and often roofs beyond a certain pitch are commonly excluded from a standard inspection unless specifically requested and paid for separately. Buyers who assume a standard inspection is comprehensive are often missing exactly the systems most likely to produce a five-figure surprise.

The Negotiation Tactic Almost Nobody Uses

Most buyers negotiate price and repairs. Few negotiate the closing timeline itself, which can be worth real money to a motivated seller and cost you nothing extra to request. A seller who needs to close quickly, or needs extra time to move out, will often trade meaningfully on price or repair credits for the timeline flexibility that costs you nothing to offer.

A Final, Uncomfortable Question Worth Sitting With

"Am I buying this because it's genuinely right for my life, or because I'm exhausted from looking and this is good enough?" Buyer fatigue is real, and it produces more regretted purchases than almost any other single factor in the process. The house that's "good enough" after month four of searching rarely stays feeling good enough once the exhaustion of searching has worn off and ordinary life resumes inside it.

What Real Estate Attorneys Wish Buyers Asked Earlier

Attorneys who handle real estate closings consistently report that the disputes they see most often trace back to questions buyers assumed were covered by standard practice but were never actually confirmed in writing — who pays for a specific repair, what exact fixtures convey with the sale, what happens to a deposit if financing falls through at the last moment. Assuming "standard practice" covers you is one of the more expensive assumptions a buyer can make.

A Reflection Before Your Final Offer

It's worth asking honestly: have I actually read every document I'm about to sign, or am I trusting that my agent and attorney caught everything? Both professionals are genuinely useful, and neither replaces your own understanding of a transaction this large. The buyers who report the fewest regrets are consistently the ones who read every page themselves, uncomfortable and time-consuming as that is, rather than delegating full trust to professionals with their own competing incentives.

A Closing Word on Trusting the Process Without Rushing It

Every uncomfortable question in this piece takes a small amount of courage to ask, in the moment, of a professional whose approval you may instinctively want. That discomfort is genuinely worth pushing through. The buyers who ask every uncomfortable question upfront consistently report fewer regrets a year later than the buyers who stayed polite throughout and found out the hard way what those questions would have revealed.
