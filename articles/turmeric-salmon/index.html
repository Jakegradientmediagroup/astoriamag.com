One-Pan Turmeric Salmon with Broccoli & Sweet Potato

By Astoria Editors · 5 min read · Wellness & Body

Some weeknight dinners require a genuine plan. This one requires a sheet pan, eight ingredients, and about thirty minutes of mostly unattended time — which is exactly the kind of math that makes a recipe worth keeping in permanent rotation.

Salmon, broccoli, and sweet potato roast together on one pan, seasoned simply enough that the anti-inflammatory ingredients get to do their actual work rather than hiding under a dozen competing flavors. It's the kind of dinner that quietly supports everything we wrote about in our piece on eating for calm — without asking you to think about nutrition science at 6pm on a Tuesday.

What You'll Need

Serves 4 · Prep 10 min · Cook 22 min · Total 32 min

  • 4 salmon fillets (about 6 oz each)
  • 4 cups broccoli florets
  • 2 cups sweet potato, cut into ½-inch cubes
  • 3 tbsp olive oil
  • 2 garlic cloves, minced
  • ½ tsp ground turmeric
  • ½ tsp salt
  • ¼ tsp black pepper

How to Make It

1. Start the sweet potato

Preheat the oven to 425°F (220°C). Toss the sweet potato with half the olive oil, half the garlic, the turmeric, salt, and pepper directly on a sheet pan.

2. Give it a head start

Roast the sweet potato alone for about 10 minutes. This is the step that keeps everything finishing at the same time later — sweet potato simply takes longer than salmon or broccoli, so it needs the extra runway.

3. Add the broccoli and salmon

Push the sweet potato to one side of the pan. Toss the broccoli with the remaining olive oil and garlic, then add it to the pan alongside the salmon fillets. Season the salmon with a pinch of salt and pepper.

4. Finish roasting

Return everything to the oven for about 12 more minutes, until the salmon flakes easily with a fork and the broccoli is tender-crisp.

"Cut the sweet potato into small, even ½-inch cubes so it finishes cooking at the same time as the salmon and broccoli — this one detail is what keeps the whole pan honest."

Why It's Actually Anti-Inflammatory

This isn't just a nice-sounding label. Each ingredient here is doing real, specific work:

  • Salmon is rich in omega-3 fatty acids, which help lower inflammatory markers in the body.
  • Turmeric and black pepper, used together, matter more than either alone — the pepper significantly boosts absorption of curcumin, turmeric's active anti-inflammatory compound.
  • Olive oil contains oleocanthal, a polyphenol with effects similar to ibuprofen.
  • Broccoli adds sulforaphane, a compound shown to help reduce inflammation.

None of this requires you to think like a nutritionist while you cook. It just requires this exact pan, on a night when you want dinner to quietly work in your favor instead of against you.

Swaps and Substitutions

This recipe is forgiving, which is exactly what a weeknight dinner should be. A few reliable swaps if you're working with what's already in your fridge:

  • No sweet potato? Regular potatoes, butternut squash, or even carrots roast beautifully in the same window and pick up the turmeric just as well.
  • No broccoli? Brussels sprouts, cauliflower, or green beans all hold up to the same roasting time with only minor timing adjustments.
  • Dairy-free, gluten-free, low-carb — this recipe already checks all three boxes as written, which makes it one of the easier things to serve when you're cooking for a mixed table of dietary needs.

Make It Ahead

The vegetables hold up well in the refrigerator for up to four days, which makes this a genuinely useful recipe to double on a Sunday. Roast a second batch of sweet potato and broccoli alongside the first, then reheat with freshly cooked salmon later in the week rather than trying to reheat the fish itself — salmon is best cooked fresh each time, since reheating tends to dry it out and push past that ideal flake.

If you do need to reheat leftover salmon, a low oven (around 275°F) for eight to ten minutes covered loosely with foil keeps it from drying out far better than a microwave will.

What to Serve Alongside

This is genuinely a complete meal on its own, but if you're feeding a bigger table or want something to stretch it further, a simple bed of rice or quinoa underneath soaks up the pan juices well. A squeeze of lemon over everything right before serving brightens the whole plate more than any single ingredient added during cooking could.

A Note on the Salmon Itself

Wild-caught salmon will generally have a slightly firmer texture and a deeper color than farm-raised, and either works fine here — this recipe doesn't require anything particularly special from the fish itself. What matters more is not overcooking it. Pull the pan the moment the thickest part of the fillet flakes easily with a fork; salmon continues cooking slightly even after it leaves the oven, and a fillet that looks slightly underdone at the very center when you pull it will usually finish perfectly by the time it reaches the table.
