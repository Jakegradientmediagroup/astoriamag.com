I Stopped Trying to Be My Kids' Friend and Became a Better Mother Because of It

By Kate Brennan · 7 min read · Motherhood

For the first several years of parenting, I operated on an assumption I never actually examined out loud: that being liked by my children, moment to moment, was a reasonable proxy for doing a good job as their mother. If they were happy with me, I must be getting it right. If they were upset with a boundary I'd set, some part of me quietly registered it as a failure on my part, something to be smoothed over quickly, apologized for, undone if possible.

I want to walk through how I discovered this assumption was not just wrong, but actively working against the kind of mother I actually wanted to be, and what changed once I finally let it go.

The Moment I Noticed the Pattern

It became visible to me during a fairly ordinary conflict — my son, then eight, was furious about screen time being cut off, and I found myself, mid-conflict, negotiating with him in real time, extending the deadline, softening the consequence, essentially trying to argue my way back into his good graces before the argument had even finished. I remember catching myself and thinking: I am not currently parenting. I am campaigning for his approval, in the middle of an interaction where I am supposed to be the adult holding a boundary.

Where I Think This Instinct Actually Comes From

I don't think this instinct is unique to me, and I don't think it's random. A lot of women, myself included, grew up in a culture that trains us from very early on to prioritize being liked over being respected, being agreeable over being clear. That training doesn't disappear the moment we become mothers. It just finds a new target. Instead of seeking approval from a boss, a partner, or a friend group, I'd started unconsciously seeking it from my own eight-year-old, in a dynamic where he had no business being the one whose approval I needed to function.

"I had spent years training my son that his displeasure was a problem I needed to solve immediately, which is precisely backward from what an eight-year-old actually needs to learn about how the world works."

What I Was Actually Teaching Him, Without Meaning To

Here's the part that genuinely alarmed me once I saw it clearly: by treating his anger at a boundary as something urgent for me to fix, I was teaching him, in real time, that other people's boundaries are negotiable if he simply expresses enough displeasure about them. That is a genuinely dangerous lesson to hand an eight-year-old boy who will grow into a teenage boy, then a young man, who will eventually be in relationships, workplaces, and friendships where that exact lesson — that boundaries dissolve under enough of my displeasure — will actively harm him and the people around him.

The Distinction I Had to Build For Myself

I had to build an actual working distinction between being warm and being agreeable, because I'd been quietly conflating the two. Warmth, I decided, meant genuine affection, real listening, physical comfort, humor, presence. Agreeableness meant bending a decision because someone was upset about it. I could keep all of the first category fully intact — I never wanted to become a cold or distant mother — while deliberately removing the second, which had nothing to do with actual warmth and everything to do with my own discomfort with being the source of someone else's unhappiness, even briefly.

What this looked like in an actual conflict

The next time a similar screen-time conflict happened, I stayed warm — I acknowledged his anger directly, said I understood he was upset, sat with him through the feeling rather than dismissing it — while keeping the actual boundary completely unchanged. This was, I want to be honest, much harder than either of my old patterns. It's easier to be purely permissive or purely rigid than to hold both warmth and firmness at the same time. But it's the only version that seems to actually work.

What Changed for Him, Not Just for Me

Within a few months, something genuinely shifted in how my son argued with me. The negotiating tactics — the escalating displeasure clearly aimed at wearing me down — started to fade, not because I'd punished them out of him, but because they'd simply stopped working, and children are remarkably efficient at abandoning strategies that no longer produce results. What replaced it wasn't compliance exactly. It was something closer to a more honest kind of negotiation, where he'd make an actual case for something rather than simply escalating an emotional display, because he'd learned that the emotional display alone was no longer the lever that moved me.

The Research That Confirmed What I Was Noticing

Developmental psychologists distinguish between "permissive" parenting, where warmth is high but boundaries are inconsistent or negotiable, and "authoritative" parenting, where warmth stays high but boundaries remain firm and consistently enforced. The research consistently finds that children raised in the authoritative style tend to develop better emotional regulation, better peer relationships, and paradoxically, report feeling more loved and secure than children raised in the permissive style, where the constant renegotiation of boundaries can actually read to a child, at some subconscious level, as instability rather than freedom.

I didn't need the research to know something had shifted for my son. But finding it after the fact was validating in a way I didn't expect — confirmation that what felt like a harder, less immediately popular parenting choice was actually the one more associated with long-term security, not less.

A Provocative Thought About Friendship and Parenting

I've come to believe that "being my kids' friend" is a phrase that sounds warm and actually describes something closer to abdication. A friend has no authority over you and no responsibility for your long-term development. A mother has both, and pretending otherwise, in the name of a warmer relationship, actually shortchanges the child of the one thing a friend structurally cannot provide: a consistent boundary, held by someone who loves them enough to tolerate their temporary anger about it.

What This Doesn't Mean

I want to be clear this isn't an argument for coldness, rigidity, or authoritarian parenting, which the same research shows produces its own set of problems — children who comply out of fear rather than respect, and who often struggle with their own boundary-setting later in life, having never been modeled one that came with warmth attached. The goal isn't less warmth. It's warmth that doesn't collapse under pressure, which turns out to be a much harder skill to build than either pure warmth or pure firmness alone.

A Closing Word

My son is eleven now, and we are, by any measure I can see, close — he talks to me, seeks me out, still wants my company voluntarily, which was the actual thing I was afraid I'd lose by holding firmer boundaries all those years ago. What I've learned is that the closeness was never actually at risk from the boundaries. It was at risk from the version of me who couldn't tell the difference between being loved and being agreed with, and who kept trying to solve his temporary displeasure instead of simply letting him feel it, safely, in the presence of someone steady enough not to need him to feel differently right away.

What I Had to Grieve in Myself to Get Here

I want to be honest that this shift required grieving something in myself, not just correcting a parenting technique. The part of me that needed my son's immediate approval wasn't only a parenting habit. It was connected to a much older pattern of needing to be liked in every relationship I'd ever had, going back to my own childhood. Choosing to hold a boundary and tolerate his temporary anger meant tolerating an old, familiar discomfort in myself that had nothing to do with him at all. Some days I still feel the pull toward the old pattern, particularly when I'm tired or already stretched thin in other parts of my life. The difference now is that I recognize the pull for what it is — my own old wound, not an accurate signal that something has gone wrong in the parenting moment itself.
