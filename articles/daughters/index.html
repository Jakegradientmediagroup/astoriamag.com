Raising Confident Daughters in an Age That Wants to Confuse Them

By Kate Brennan · 9 min read · Relationships & Marriage

My daughter is eleven. She is, as my mother would say, fully herself — opinionated, funny, direct, secure in a way that I was not at her age and am still working toward at thirty-eight. I am not writing this to tell you I have it figured out. I'm writing it because I spend a lot of time thinking about what it means to raise a girl right now, and the more I talk to other mothers, the more I believe we're all asking the same questions and not getting enough honest answers.

Identity Before Opinion

The most important thing you can give a daughter before the world gets loud around her is a strong, stable sense of who she is — separate from what she produces, how she looks, or what anyone thinks of her. This comes first from being known inside your family. From having her ideas taken seriously at the dinner table. From seeing her mother model the kind of woman she's allowed to become.

The Body Conversation That Has to Come First

Before any other conversation about her body, your daughter needs to hear, clearly and repeatedly, that her body is good and it belongs to her. Not as an object to be perfected or criticized or compared. As a home she lives in. This matters especially in families with a strong wellness culture, which can inadvertently communicate that food and exercise are moral categories. They're not.

"A girl who knows she is loved unconditionally is much harder to manipulate than one who believes love must be earned."

Faith as Foundation, Not Performance

For families raising daughters in faith: there is a difference between a faith that roots a child and a faith that is performed for social belonging. Children can feel the difference. The faith that protects a daughter is the one that is genuinely hers — the one she has questioned and returned to, the one she sees lived out in the texture of her family's daily life.

The Conversation About Men You Have to Keep Having

Not a lecture. A conversation, and an ongoing one. What she deserves from the men and boys in her life. How to distinguish between someone who values her and someone who values what she offers him. Tell her, specifically and often, what you see in her — not "you're so pretty" but the particular things. The way she stood up for her friend last week. The question she asked that nobody else thought to ask.
