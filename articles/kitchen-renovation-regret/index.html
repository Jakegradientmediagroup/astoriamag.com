I Renovated My Kitchen Twice Trying to Make It “Instagram-Worthy.” The Third Time, I Finally Got It Right

By Isabelle Clarke · 8 min read · Home & Design
▣

The first kitchen renovation cost just under sixty thousand dollars, and I hated it within four months. The second one, an attempt to fix the first, cost another twenty-two thousand, and I hated that one too, just for different reasons. It wasn't until the third pass — smaller, cheaper, and built around an entirely different question — that I finally ended up with a kitchen I actually wanted to be in. I want to walk through what went wrong the first two times, because I think an enormous number of women are about to make the exact mistakes I made, for the exact same reasons.

What I Was Actually Designing For, Without Realizing It

The first renovation was designed, if I'm honest with myself, almost entirely for photographs. I had a folder of saved images — waterfall quartz islands, brass fixtures, a specific shade of white oak flooring — and I built toward that folder with very little consideration for how my actual family of five used a kitchen on an ordinary Tuesday. The island was a showpiece, gorgeous and photogenic, and also somehow always covered in mail, backpacks, and the general debris of family life within about six weeks, because I hadn't built in a single dedicated place for any of that debris to actually live.

I didn't realize until much later that I'd designed a kitchen for an audience that didn't exist — the imagined visitor who'd walk in and be impressed — rather than for the five people who actually used the room every single day.

The Uncomfortable Truth About Renovating for an Aesthetic

Here's what I think is genuinely true and rarely said plainly in home design content: a kitchen built primarily to look good in photographs and a kitchen built to function well for a real family are frequently in direct tension with each other, not gentle alignment. Open shelving photographs beautifully and is a genuine nightmare for anyone who cooks daily, since everything on it needs to look curated at all times or the whole effect collapses. A single oversized island looks stunning in a listing photo and creates a genuine traffic jam in a kitchen where multiple people are trying to move at once during a real Tuesday dinner rush.

"I had built a kitchen that looked like someone's life, instead of building one that fit mine."

The Second Renovation: Solving the Wrong Problem Again

When I gutted and redid it the second time, I told myself I was fixing the functional problems. In some ways I was — better storage, a proper mudroom drop zone, more realistic material choices. But I was still, underneath it, designing toward an aesthetic ideal rather than starting from how we actually lived. I added a second island specifically because a design magazine had shown one, not because we needed it. I still hadn't asked the actual question that would have saved me both renovations and roughly forty thousand dollars.

The Question That Finally Changed Everything

The question, when I finally landed on it during the third and final pass, was embarrassingly simple: what do the five specific people who live in this house actually do in this room, at what times of day, and what does each of them need in order to do that well? Not what a stranger admiring a photograph would need. What my husband, who makes coffee at 5:45am before anyone else is awake, needs. What my teenager, who does homework at the counter every single afternoon, needs. What I need at 6pm when three people want three different things from me simultaneously.

What this actually looked like in practice

A dedicated, unglamorous coffee station near the door my husband uses first thing, so he's not navigating around anyone. A specific stretch of counter, deliberately away from the cooking zone, built for homework and laptops, with an outlet exactly where a charging cord needed to reach. Deep, closed storage instead of open shelving, because I finally admitted to myself that I did not want to keep anything visually curated at 6pm on a Wednesday. None of this photographs particularly well. All of it works.

What I'd Tell a Woman About to Start Her First Renovation

Before you save a single more image to a design board, sit down and write out an honest, unglamorous hour-by-hour account of what actually happens in your kitchen on a real Tuesday. Not an aspirational Tuesday. The real one, mail pile and all. Design toward that document, not toward the folder of saved photographs. The photographs will always look better than your real life. That was never a fair comparison to design against.

What Sixty Thousand Dollars Actually Bought Me, Eventually

Across three renovations and roughly a hundred thousand dollars total, here is what I actually learned, which I could have learned for the cost of one honest conversation with myself: the version of your home that other people are supposed to admire, and the version that's supposed to hold your actual life, are not automatically the same project. Sometimes they overlap. Often they don't. And building for the wrong one, twice, taught me more expensively than I'd wish on anyone that the second kind is the one worth spending the money on.

A Closing Word

My kitchen now is quieter, less photogenic, and considerably more expensive than it needed to be, given how simple the final version actually is. If I could go back, I'd skip the first two renovations entirely and just ask the real question from the start. I share this not to be dramatic about a kitchen, of all things, but because I think the same mistake — designing a life for the imagined audience instead of the real people living it — shows up in a lot more rooms of our lives than just this one.

What Design Psychology Actually Says About This

Environmental psychologists who study how people actually use domestic space have a term for the gap I fell into twice: designing for "display use" rather than "lived use." Display use is how a space performs for an imagined observer — a guest, a photographer, a stranger scrolling past on social media. Lived use is how the people who actually inhabit the space move through it, day after ordinary day, when no one is watching or photographing anything. The research consistently shows that homes designed heavily around display use tend to produce lower daily satisfaction for the people living in them, even when those same homes generate the most compliments from visitors and the most engagement on social platforms. The compliments and the daily satisfaction are, frustratingly, not the same currency.

This tracks exactly with my experience. My first kitchen generated more compliments from visitors than my third one ever will. My third kitchen is the only one I've ever actually enjoyed being in at 6pm on an ordinary Wednesday, which turned out to be a far more valuable thing to optimize for, given how many more Wednesdays I live through than dinner parties.

The Financial Reckoning I Had to Have With Myself

I want to be honest about the money here, because I think glossing over it does a disservice to anyone reading this while planning their own renovation. Between the three passes, I spent roughly double what a single, well-considered renovation would have cost if I'd asked the right question from the beginning. That's a genuinely painful number to sit with, and no amount of "well, now I've learned something" fully erases the fact that I could have put that difference toward almost anything else — college funds, a second property, a decade of family vacations.

What I've made peace with is that the lesson itself, expensive as it was to learn, has since saved me from making the same mistake in every other major purchase decision since — the car we bought, the furniture we chose, even how we planned a recent family trip. Once you see the display-use-versus-lived-use pattern clearly in one area of your life, you start noticing it everywhere, and that pattern recognition has arguably paid for itself many times over in decisions made since.

A practical filter I now use before any purchase

Before any significant home decision now, I ask one filtering question: am I picturing myself using this, or am I picturing someone else seeing it? If the honest answer is the second one, I know I need to slow down and ask the harder question before spending another dollar.

What My Daughters Have Noticed, Without Me Saying Anything

The unexpected postscript to this whole expensive saga is that my two daughters, without any prompting from me, have both commented that the current kitchen "feels like ours" in a way the first one apparently didn't, even though they were too young at the time to articulate why. I never told them about the display-use research, or the three renovations, or any of the internal reasoning. They simply noticed, in whatever way children notice these things, that a space built around their actual homework habits and actual snack routines felt more like home than a space built around a photograph. That's the piece of evidence, more than any spreadsheet or research citation, that has actually convinced me I got the third version right.

A Closing Word on Sunk Cost and Self-Forgiveness

I've talked to enough women who've made similarly expensive mistakes in their own homes to know that the hardest part usually isn't the design lesson itself. It's forgiving yourself for the money already spent learning it. I don't have a tidy resolution for that discomfort beyond this: the version of me who built the first two kitchens didn't have access to the question that finally worked. She was doing her best with the information and the design culture she'd absorbed, which at the time treated a photographed kitchen as the obvious goal. Holding some compassion for her, rather than only frustration, has made the actual dollars easier to sit with than the shame alone ever did.
