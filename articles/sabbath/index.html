I Didn't Answer a Single Text for 24 Hours. Here's What It Did to My Marriage and My Mind

By Anna Collins · 5 min read · Faith & Community

I grew up in a home where Sunday was genuinely different from every other day. Slower meals. No errands. A long walk in the afternoon. My father reading in a chair without appearing to feel guilty about it. I didn't have language for what I was experiencing, but I know now that I was watching people practice Sabbath, and that it shaped my understanding of rest in ways I'm still discovering.

What Rest Is Not

Rest is not the absence of activity. Scrolling your phone for three hours is the absence of activity; it is not rest. Rest, in the rich theological and anthropological sense of the word, is a positive practice. It is the deliberate creation of space for replenishment — not just of energy, but of perspective, of joy, of the kind of presence that allows you to be genuinely available to the people who need you.

The Physiology of Why This Matters

The nervous system that drives high performance requires periodic and genuine deactivation to remain functional. People who take genuine weekly rest periods — not just lower-intensity work, but true disengagement — outperform their continuously working counterparts on virtually every cognitive and performance measure over time. The Sabbath principle isn't just theological. It's physiological.

"Rest is not what you do when the work is finished. The work is never finished. Rest is what you choose, in defiance of that fact, because you understand your own humanity."

What a Sabbath Practice Can Look Like in a Modern Life

It does not require a specific theology. What it requires is a consistent, weekly period — ideally 24 hours, at minimum a full afternoon and evening — during which you do not engage in the activities that constitute your work week. No email. No errands that feel like obligations.

Start small. Choose one afternoon. Protect it as seriously as you protect a meeting that cannot be rescheduled. Notice what it does to the rest of your week. Then expand it slowly as your capacity for rest — which, like any atrophied muscle, needs to be rebuilt — grows back.

Rest and community tend to reinforce each other — for more on the relationship side of that equation, read how to build a social circle that actually nourishes you.

The Uncomfortable Truth About Why Rest Feels So Hard to Actually Take

For many high-achieving women, the resistance to rest isn't really about time scarcity. It's about a deeper, often unexamined belief that rest has to be earned through sufficient output first — a belief that means true rest never actually arrives, because there's always more output that could theoretically be produced first. This is worth naming plainly, because no amount of calendar rearranging fixes a resistance to rest that's actually rooted in a worth-through-productivity belief operating underneath the schedule.

The Provocative Part: Some "Rest" Is Actually Avoidance

Not everything that looks like rest functions like rest. Hours of passive scrolling, binge-watching used to numb rather than restore, can look identical to genuine rest from the outside while doing something closer to the opposite internally — leaving you more depleted, not less, once it ends. Genuine rest requires enough presence to actually notice you're resting. Numbing requires the opposite: enough distraction to avoid noticing anything at all.

"The test isn't whether you stopped working. It's whether you feel more like yourself afterward, or less."

What the Research on Sabbath-Style Rest Actually Shows

Recovery requires genuine disengagement, not just reduced intensity

Working at 50% intensity all weekend isn't recovery, physiologically. The nervous system needs a genuine break from the specific demand pattern, not just a quieter version of the same pattern continued at lower volume.

The benefits compound over weeks, not single instances

A single day of rest after months of depletion won't undo the depletion. The research on sabbath-style rest patterns shows its benefits accumulate over consistent weeks and months — meaning the first few attempts may not feel transformative, and that's not evidence it isn't working.

Practical Ways to Tell Real Rest From Avoidance

Ask how you feel afterward, honestly

Genuine rest leaves you with more capacity than before. Avoidance-based "rest" often leaves a low-grade residue of guilt or a sense of time having disappeared without anything to show for the feeling of having rested.

Notice whether you can stop without external permission

A woman who can only rest once every task is finished, every email answered, will never actually rest, because that state of completion doesn't arrive in most modern lives. Genuine sabbath-style rest requires practicing the decision to stop while things remain genuinely unfinished — which is exactly the uncomfortable, countercultural part.

What Occupational Health Research Actually Shows

Studies on burnout consistently find that the recovery variable isn't total hours worked, but the presence or absence of genuine psychological detachment during off-hours — the ability to stop thinking about work entirely, not just stop physically doing it. Many women who technically take a day off remain in a state of partial mental engagement the entire time, checking email "just once," mentally drafting tomorrow's to-do list. This partial engagement prevents the actual recovery that full detachment provides, even when the hours logged look identical from the outside.

A Reflection on What's Actually Stopping You

It's worth asking honestly: if I fully rested for twenty-four hours, what specifically am I afraid would happen? For many women, the honest answer involves a fear of falling behind, of being seen as less committed, or of losing a sense of control that constant motion provides. Naming that specific fear directly tends to be more useful than another article about the benefits of rest, because the fear, not a lack of information, is usually the actual obstacle.

A Closing Word on Starting Smaller Than You Want To

If a full 24-hour Sabbath feels impossible in your current season, the answer isn't to abandon the practice. It's to shrink it until it fits: two genuinely disengaged hours instead of a full day, once a week, protected as seriously as anything else on your calendar. A small, consistent version of real rest will do more for your actual nervous system over a year than an ambitious, once-attempted full day that collapses under its own weight by the second week.
