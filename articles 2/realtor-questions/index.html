First Home or Fifth: The Questions Every Smart Woman Asks Her Realtor

By Dana Whitmore · 8 min read · Real Estate & Home

Real estate transactions are among the largest financial decisions most people will ever make, and they are conducted in an environment specifically designed to create urgency and suppress deliberation. Everybody in the room has a financial incentive that may or may not align with yours. This is a reason to be prepared.

Questions for Your Buyer's Agent

"What's the actual price per square foot for comparable properties sold in the last 90 days?"

Not listed. Sold. Listing prices are aspirations. Sale prices are data. Any agent worth working with can pull this in five minutes and should offer it before you make an offer.

"Why are the sellers moving?"

Motivated sellers negotiate differently than sellers testing the market. Your agent may not always know, but asking the question reveals how much information they've bothered to gather.

"What are this neighborhood's long-term trajectory and most significant risk factors?"

A good agent has opinions about where neighborhoods are heading and is willing to share them honestly. If they answer every version of this question with unqualified enthusiasm, they're telling you they prioritize the sale over your interests.

"The right question isn't 'should I buy this house?' It's 'what would I need to know to feel confident about this decision?' Then find out everything on that list."

Questions for the Inspection

Ask the inspector to walk you through the roof, HVAC, and foundation in plain language. These are the three systems most likely to produce a large, unplanned expense in the first five years. Ask what they'd do if it were their house.

The Question Nobody Asks

"If I buy this property and need to sell it in three years, what's my realistic outcome?" This forces a realistic conversation about liquidity and market risk that most buyers never have because they're too emotionally invested by the time they're considering an offer. You need to know before the closing, not after.
